01 — Context & Challenge

The Research Problem

Online asynchronous courses at scale depend on the validity of their assessments. A program perceived as easy to cheat in cannot recruit serious students, and faculty cannot teach into it with confidence. Keeping the assessments — and the program — credible was therefore both a research problem and a business problem at once.

Within that frame, the program operated under a dual mandate: dissuade broadly enough to keep the courses healthy, and catch the holdouts with evidence strong enough to survive formal review. Everything below follows from holding both halves of that mandate at the same time.

  • Program objectives Dissuade students from cheating at scale; catch — with defensible confidence — those who cheated anyway.
  • Research question How do we distinguish coordinated cheating from coincidental overlap across hundreds of assessments and thousands of students, at signal levels usable for both broad behavioral intervention and formal institutional proceedings?
  • Desired outcomes Dissuasion lists that shrink across each semester; a small set of escalations that hold up to formal review; an online program credible enough — internally and externally — to keep attracting students.

02 — Research Design

A Two-Stage Mixed-Methods Funnel

The dual goal — dissuade at scale, catch with confidence — required two different kinds of evidence at two different evidentiary bars. Quantitative methods alone could screen a large population efficiently but could not establish whether a flagged pattern was actually coordinated behavior or coincidental overlap. Qualitative methods alone could establish meaningful behavioral patterns case by case but could not be applied at the scale needed to surface candidates in the first place.

Mixed methods solved both problems by sequencing them. Stage one — quantitative — produced the broad list of students whose behavior was unusual enough to warrant intervention. Stage two — qualitative — produced the case-level confidence required to escalate a subset of those students to formal proceedings. Each stage served a different research purpose, and each stage produced a different deliverable for a different stakeholder.

Stage 1

Quantitative Screening — Who to Examine

The goal of Stage 1 was to reduce a population of thousands of student pairs to a manageable set warranting deeper qualitative investigation. We deliberately chose the K-Index and the Time-Coincidence Index because they are the field-standard metrics in the assessment-integrity literature — the same family of statistics used by organizations like ETS to flag suspicious behavior on high-stakes standardized exams such as the SAT. Adopting established metrics meant our findings were legible and defensible to outside stakeholders who already understood what those numbers meant.

K-Index
How unlikely is it that two students got the same questions wrong, in the same way, by chance?
Computes the probability that two students would share as many identical wrong answers as observed, given their individual performance levels. Wrong answers are the signal: students who know the material naturally converge on correct answers, but shared errors are harder to explain innocently.
Time-Coincidence Index
How unlikely is it that two students were on the same question at the same moment, over and over, by chance?
Uses permutation testing (10,000 iterations per pair) to establish a baseline distribution of how often two independently working students would be expected to answer the same question within a given time window. Pairs whose observed co-timing fell outside this distribution were flagged.

Both metrics were deliberately calibrated to be conservative — erring toward under-flagging rather than over-flagging, because the consequences of a false positive were serious for students and faculty alike.

Stage 2

Qualitative Visual Analysis — Whether the Pattern Made Sense

Quantitative thresholds determined who to examine more closely. Qualitative analysis determined whether the observed pattern was meaningful. This involved visual inspection of each student pair's exam-navigation timeline — a graphical representation of which questions each student was viewing at each moment in time, compared side by side.

The key behavioral signals assessed in this stage included:

  • Simultaneous tab switching — two students moving to the same question at the same time despite randomized question ordering
  • Answer switching — one student changing their response to match the other student's answer after the other had submitted
  • Following behavior — one student's navigation pattern mirroring the other's across an entire assessment
  • Response timing clustering — bursts of coordinated activity concentrated in specific windows of the exam

This qualitative methodology evolved significantly over three years. The most important discovery was answer switching — a behavior in which one student would change their response to match the other's after the other had submitted. Answer switching was largely invisible to the quantitative metrics, which measured answer agreement at final submission rather than tracking changes over time. Identifying and standardizing this as an additional qualitative indicator substantially improved the accuracy of the overall process.

This heuristic framework was documented and trained into three graduate research assistants, four faculty clients, and presented in a methodology demonstration for university leadership.

03 — From Findings to Action

Two Deliverables, Two Stakeholders, One Pipeline

Two deliverables sat at the operational core of the program, and together they encoded the dissuade-and-catch pipeline in artifact form. The first defined who to dissuade. The second defined who we caught, and only ever drew from students who had first appeared in the dissuasion stream. Both were required because they did different work, for different stakeholders, at different evidentiary bars.

For — Faculty Clients

Weekly Dissuasion Reports

What they did: defined who to dissuade. Each week's report named the students whose quantitative metrics had crossed our flagging thresholds, so faculty could send academic-warning communications, increase monitoring, or otherwise raise the cost of continued behavior.

Evidentiary bar: lower. The goal was behavioral intervention, not punishment — the reports needed to be accurate enough to warrant a faculty contact, not airtight enough to support a formal proceeding.

For — Student Judicial Services

Formal Evidential Memos

What they did: defined who we caught. Each memo built a complete case against a specific student or pair, combining quantitative p-values, qualitative behavioral analysis, methodology explanation, and annotated visual exhibits into a single defensible document.

Evidentiary bar: high. The statistical case had to be airtight and the visual evidence had to be legible to a non-specialist audience who would adjudicate the matter.

The reports fed the memos. A student rarely made it into an evidential memo without first appearing on multiple dissuasion reports across the semester. That sequencing was deliberate: by the time a case reached Student Judicial Services, it had already survived several rounds of softer intervention and continued to meet the criteria nonetheless. That cumulative record — quantitative flags week after week, plus the qualitative behavioral pattern — was what made the formal cases defensible.
04 — Impact

Behavioral Change at Scale

Headline Impact

I defined the standards that let this program scale.

Before I took control of the program, the analytical methods existed — but they were applied without consistent, articulated heuristics for who belonged in the dissuasion list versus who warranted escalation to a formal evidential memo. The boundary between the two was implicit and varied case by case.

I defined that boundary explicitly: the quantitative thresholds for flagging, the qualitative criteria for escalation, and the handoff logic that connected one category to the other. Once those standards were articulated, the methodology became something that could be applied uniformly across courses, semesters, and team members — and trained into other people. That is what allowed the program to grow during my tenure across 7–10 courses per semester, three graduate research assistants, and four faculty clients, while staying internally consistent.

The primary impact metric for this program was not the number of students escalated to formal proceedings — that data was appropriately confidential — but rather the trajectory of the dissuasion list over each semester.

05 — Key Takeaways

What This Program Proved

Two findings stand out as the program's primary takeaways — one about the system as built, one about what I added to it along the way.

Takeaway 1

The system worked as designed: dissuade broadly, catch narrowly.

The two-stage funnel did what it was meant to do. The quantitative stage drove dissuasion at scale — as flagged students were contacted and monitored, dissuasion lists consistently shrank over the course of each semester. That trajectory was direct evidence that the dissuasion half of the mandate was producing measurable behavioral change in the flagged population.

The qualitative stage then performed its complementary function: it weeded out students whose quantitative flags were not borne out by closer behavioral inspection, so that the cases escalated to formal proceedings were only those for which the evidence was strong on both axes. We dissuaded broadly and caught narrowly — by design, not by accident.

Takeaway 2

Standardizing answer switching into the workflow raised our qualitative confidence.

Over time, as the team ran the qualitative visual analysis across many assessments, we came to recognize answer switching — a student changing their final response to match another student's after that student had submitted — as a uniquely useful behavioral signal. It was largely invisible to the quantitative metrics, which captured agreement only at the moment of final submission rather than the path of changes that got students there.

My contribution was operational. I incorporated answer switching into the standard qualitative workflow — defining what it looked like on the navigation timeline, when it warranted escalation, and how to document it for an evidential memo. Once it was part of the standard work rather than a signal we sometimes happened to notice, the qualitative stage had a second reinforcing signal alongside tab-switching alignment, and we could speak about coordinated behavior with materially more confidence than we could before.

06 — Reflection

What This Work Taught Me

On the boundary between statistical evidence and human judgment

The most intellectually interesting challenge in this work was navigating the hand-off point between quantitative and qualitative analysis. The statistical metrics were designed to be conservative — to minimize false positives — but they were also necessarily incomplete. Numbers can tell you that something unusual happened; they cannot always tell you what it means.

The qualitative stage existed precisely to answer the question the numbers couldn't: does this pattern make sense as human behavior? Learning to read behavioral timelines — to develop and then articulate a "trained eye" — required building a mental model of how students normally move through an exam, so that deviation from that pattern became legible. That is not a statistical skill. It is a research skill: the ability to hold a model of normal behavior in mind and recognize when observed behavior departs from it in a meaningful way.

The discovery of answer switching as a distinct behavioral signal was a product of that qualitative rigor. It emerged from close observation of patterns the numbers flagged but couldn't explain — and it substantially changed how the program operated. That experience reinforced something I carry into all research work: quantitative analysis tells you where to look; qualitative analysis tells you what you're seeing.
