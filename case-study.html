01 — Context & Challenge

The Research Problem

Online asynchronous courses at scale create a fundamental behavioral research problem: how do you distinguish normal variation in student exam behavior from coordinated action, across hundreds of assessments and thousands of students, while minimizing false positives that could harm innocent students?

Conventional monitoring tools flag surface-level similarities but cannot account for the statistical base rate of coincidental answer overlap. At the same time, the stakes of over-flagging are significant — accusing a student of academic dishonesty on insufficient evidence undermines the integrity of the process itself.

The challenge was to design a rigorous, defensible, and scalable research methodology: one that could produce findings reliable enough to inform both faculty intervention and formal institutional proceedings, while remaining calibrated to minimize false positives.

This work was fundamentally about keeping online classes valid. Because the courses were large and asynchronous, maintaining assessment integrity was essential to ensuring that all students had a genuine opportunity to learn — and that the online program itself remained credible.

02 — Research Design

A Two-Stage Mixed-Methods Funnel

The methodology was designed as a deliberate two-stage funnel. Each stage served a different research purpose: the first reduced a large population to high-priority cases; the second determined whether the flagged behavioral patterns were meaningful.

Stage 1

Quantitative Screening — Who to Examine

The goal of Stage 1 was to reduce a population of thousands of student pairs to a manageable set warranting deeper qualitative investigation. Two complementary statistical metrics were applied to each assessment:

K-Index
Measures the probability that two students would share as many identical wrong answers as observed, given their individual performance levels. Wrong answers are the key signal: students who know the material naturally converge on correct answers, but shared errors are harder to explain innocently.
Time-Coincidence Index
Uses permutation testing (10,000 iterations per pair) to establish a baseline distribution of how often two independently working students would be expected to answer the same question within a given time window. Pairs whose observed co-timing fell outside this distribution were flagged.

Both metrics were deliberately calibrated to be conservative — erring toward under-flagging rather than over-flagging, because the consequences of a false positive were serious for students and faculty alike.

↓
Stage 2

Qualitative Visual Analysis — Whether the Pattern Made Sense

Quantitative thresholds determined who to examine more closely. Qualitative analysis determined whether the observed pattern was meaningful. This involved visual inspection of each student pair's exam-navigation timeline — a graphical representation of which questions each student was viewing at each moment in time, compared side by side.

The key behavioral signals assessed in this stage included:

  • Simultaneous tab switching — two students moving to the same question at the same time despite randomized question ordering
  • Answer switching — one student changing their response to match the other student's answer after the other had submitted
  • Following behavior — one student's navigation pattern mirroring the other's across an entire assessment
  • Response timing clustering — bursts of coordinated activity concentrated in specific windows of the exam

Each color represents a different exam question. The x-axis shows time elapsed during the assessment; the y-axis shows which question tab each student was viewing. Vertical alignment between the two charts indicates the students were viewing the same question at the same time. Black boxes highlight windows of coordinated activity that warrant escalation.

This qualitative methodology evolved significantly over three years. The most important discovery was answer switching — a behavior in which one student would change their response to match the other's after the other had submitted. Answer switching was largely invisible to the quantitative metrics, which measured answer agreement at final submission rather than tracking changes over time. Identifying and standardizing this as an additional qualitative indicator substantially improved the accuracy of the overall process.

This heuristic framework was documented and trained into three graduate research assistants, four faculty clients, and presented in a methodology demonstration for university leadership.

03 — From Findings to Action

Two Outputs, Two Stakeholder Needs

A key research communication insight that emerged over time: the same underlying findings needed to be packaged differently depending on what action they were meant to support. Two distinct output types served two distinct stakeholder groups with different evidentiary needs.

Stakeholder — Faculty Clients

Weekly Dissuasion Reports

Lower evidentiary bar. Designed to prompt behavioral intervention — flagging students for monitoring or academic warning — rather than formal punishment. Reports summarized quantitative thresholds crossed and noted the number of students meeting criteria each week.

Stakeholder — Student Judicial Services

Formal Evidential Memos

Higher evidentiary bar. Required the statistical case to be airtight and the visual evidence to be legible to a non-specialist audience. Memos combined quantitative p-values, methodology explanation, and annotated visual exhibits into a single defensible document.

This distinction was not merely presentational — it reflected a deliberate research design choice about what level of confidence was required for each type of action, and what format would allow non-researcher stakeholders to evaluate and act on the findings appropriately.

04 — Impact

Behavioral Change at Scale

The primary impact metric for this program was not the number of students escalated to formal proceedings — that data was appropriately confidential — but rather the trajectory of the dissuasion list over each semester.

Illustrative: Dissuasion List Trajectory Within a Semester

Week 3 — Initial flagged population (up to 10% of class / ~50 students)
Week 8 — Mid-semester, following intervention contact
Week 14 — End of semester (best case: near-zero escalations)

A consistently shrinking dissuasion list across a semester was the primary evidence of program efficacy: it indicated both that the intervention was working, and that the initial targeting was accurate enough to produce behavioral change in the flagged population.

Dissuasion lists consistently shrank over the course of each semester, indicating that faculty intervention informed by research findings was producing measurable changes in student behavior.

05 — Reflection

What This Work Taught Me

On the boundary between statistical evidence and human judgment

The most intellectually interesting challenge in this work was navigating the hand-off point between quantitative and qualitative analysis. The statistical metrics were designed to be conservative — to minimize false positives — but they were also necessarily incomplete. Numbers can tell you that something unusual happened; they cannot always tell you what it means.

The qualitative stage existed precisely to answer the question the numbers couldn't: does this pattern make sense as human behavior? Learning to read behavioral timelines — to develop and then articulate a "trained eye" — required building a mental model of how students normally move through an exam, so that deviation from that pattern became legible. That is not a statistical skill. It is a research skill: the ability to hold a model of normal behavior in mind and recognize when observed behavior departs from it in a meaningful way.

The discovery of answer switching as a distinct behavioral signal was a product of that qualitative rigor. It emerged from close observation of patterns the numbers flagged but couldn't explain — and it substantially changed how the program operated. That experience reinforced something I carry into all research work: quantitative analysis tells you where to look; qualitative analysis tells you what you're seeing.
